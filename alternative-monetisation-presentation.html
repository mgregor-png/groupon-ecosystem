Alternative Monetisation

Save Everywhere

Turn Groupon from a deal marketplace people visit monthly into a savings companion they open daily.

For: Martin, Becky & James  ·  March 26, 2026  ·  Goal: Double monetisation per UDV
The Problem

Groupon only gets opened when someone wants to buy something.

The highest-frequency savings apps have decoupled "reason to open" from "intent to transact." Groupon hasn't.

Why this matters

Fetch, Temu, and Shopkick users open daily because there's always something to check, earn, or discover — whether or not they're buying. Groupon sits on the other end: high-intent, low-frequency. Monthly visits instead of daily opens.

The result: monetisation depends entirely on conversion rather than engagement. To double revenue per UDV, we need to multiply the number of times people open the app — not just optimise what happens when they do.

Hidden asset: Groupon's affiliate coupons content is a frequency engine stuck on the web. People need promo codes constantly. Moving this content into the app creates a "save everywhere" utility layer — the bridge between monthly and daily.
Foundation

Groupon Bucks: The Connective Tissue

Every idea connects through one mechanism — a visible, growing balance that gives users a reason to come back even when they're not shopping.

User needs a promo code → Opens Groupon app → Sees a local deal → Books it → Earns Groupon Bucks → Comes back for next code
How Groupon Bucks ties it all together

Bucks accumulate from streaks, coupon use, code verifications, and referrals. They're spent on core deals. This creates a shared currency between the affiliate coupon world and the core deals world — without it, the two sides don't reinforce each other.

"You've earned $14.20 this month" — users check their balance even when they're not shopping. That's the habit loop.

Foundation

Genie for Mobile: The App Comes to You

Nobody remembers to open Groupon at checkout. So don't make them. Ship a Safari extension inside the Groupon app — just like Honey lives inside PayPal.

Safari Extension — Bundled Inside the Groupon App

Since iOS 15, apps can ship a Safari Web Extension inside their bundle. The user enables it once and grants website access. From then on, Groupon is there at every checkout — showing available codes and cashback rates. No separate install. This is exactly how Honey (inside PayPal) and Rakuten work on iOS today.

Detail

This is a proven pattern — Honey (bundled inside the PayPal app since Sept 2021) and Rakuten both ship Safari extensions inside their iOS apps today.

Why this changes everything:

  • Solves the "remember to open Groupon" problem completely
  • Every activation is a Groupon brand touchpoint — even if the code isn't used
  • Two incentive layers: promo codes (instant discount) + cashback (rewards) — stacks the value
  • Genie already proves the model on desktop — this brings it to mobile without a separate product
  • Turns every Groupon app install into a Genie mobile install for free

Technical detail

How the extension works under the hood:

  • Content script injected into Safari pages detects merchant domains (matching against Groupon's coupon database)
  • On checkout/cart pages, it injects a floating banner at the bottom of the viewport — non-intrusive, dismissible
  • Tapping "Apply" pastes the code into the promo field on the page (same approach as Honey)
  • "Claim Bucks" button uses a universal link to deep-link into the Groupon app with the savings amount pre-loaded
  • The app lands on a success screen showing Bucks earned + a "Follow this store" prompt + local deals

One-time setup (two steps): After installing the Groupon app, user enables the extension in Settings → Safari → Extensions, then sets Website Access to "All Websites." Two taps, done forever. The app can prompt and deep-link to these settings.

Key constraints:

  • Safari only — doesn't work in Chrome, Firefox, or in-app browsers (~55% of iOS mobile browsing uses Safari)
  • Content Security Policy — some sites with strict CSP may limit injected UI; the extension should gracefully fall back to Safari's native popup sheet
  • App Store guidelines — Section 4.4 says extensions can't include "marketing or advertising," but Honey and Rakuten are approved, so coupon-finding is accepted
Affiliate Coupons — The Frequency Engine

Make the app useful every time someone shops online.

These two ideas use content Groupon already has to drive daily app opens.

1. Follow Stores + Code Alerts

Follow your favourite retailers. Get a push when a new code drops: "Nike just posted 25% off — tap to grab it." Permission-based, so it feels like a service. Every alert opens the app — and exposes the user to local deals.

Detail

Honey's store-follow alerts drive the majority of their re-engagement. Groupon has codes for thousands of retailers — the content exists, it's just not personalised or push-enabled. This is the single lowest-effort, highest-impact move for affiliate content in the app.

POC: Follow button on retailer pages + push trigger on new code detection + "My Stores" screen. 4-week cohort test.

2. Weekly "Best Codes" Digest

Every Monday: "This week's top codes: 30% off ASOS, 25% Nike, 20% Uber Eats + 3 more." One notification, one curated landing page. Predictable cadence = habit formation.

Detail

Flipp built a $100M+ business on weekly grocery flyers alone (10M+ downloads). The principle: users learn "check Groupon Monday for the week's best codes." Could be manual initially (editor picks 7 codes), automated later. Pairs with the Friday Weekend Content for a two-beat weekly rhythm.

Core App — The Monetisation Engine

Give people reasons to open even when they're not shopping.

Four ideas that drive habitual engagement through local deals, content, and urgency.

3. Daily Deal Drop + Streak

3-5 curated deals rotate every 24 hours (app-only). Mix local deals + top online codes. Opening daily builds a streak — hit 7 days, earn Groupon Bucks. Miss a day, lose the streak.

Detail

Loss aversion is the strongest behavioural lever. Duolingo streaks → 3.6x long-term retention. Temu daily check-in → 89% interaction rate.

Local deals already have natural scarcity. No new supply needed — curate from existing inventory.

Risk: Streak rewards must be concrete (Groupon Bucks, exclusive unlock). Extrinsically motivated — the reward carries the weight.

4. "Watch This Deal" — Price & Availability Alerts

Save deals, categories, or retailers. Get notified when a deal restarts, a code improves, or a new match appears. "Spa deal under $50 near you" — Honey's Droplist meets Groupon's local inventory.

Detail

Not everyone will check daily. This captures intent-rich users who browse but don't buy. Deferred desire + permission-based alerts = highest-quality re-engagement. Each alert is a high-intent return visit.

Local deals are cyclical (restaurants rerun offers). Online codes appear constantly. Both create natural re-engagement triggers.

Core App

5. "What to Do This Weekend"

Every Friday: "Top 5 things to do in Chicago this weekend." Curated from deal inventory + editorial picks. Positions Groupon as a planning tool, not just a deal marketplace.

Detail

Content-driven engagement is lower friction than transactional. The Strategist earns $2M+/month on this model. Nobody else can say "here are 5 restaurant deals near you this weekend" with attached offers. Easiest to ship, most defensible.

Pairs with Monday codes digest: two fixed touchpoints per week as a minimum engagement baseline.

6. Last-Minute Local Deals

Fill merchant dead time — empty tables Tuesday lunch, salon gaps at 4pm. "3 spa slots left today, 60% off." Expires in hours, not weeks. The HotelTonight model for local services.

Detail

Users learn to check "what's available right now near me?" — fundamentally different, higher-frequency behaviour. Nobody else can surface this without building Groupon's merchant network from scratch.

Differentiation: Highest on the entire list. This is Groupon's true moat expressed as a product feature.

Risks & Discussion

What to watch & what to decide

Key Risks
  • Push fatigue: Multiple ideas rely on notifications. Solution: aggregate into a "Morning Brief" where possible; store alerts fire individually (permission-based).
  • App landing experience: Current app is built for purchase intent. Non-intent opens need a "savings hub" — a dedicated "Today" tab could work short-term.
  • Code quality: Broken codes erode trust fast. Post-use verification and community voting help, but baseline quality must be high.
  • Cannibalisation: Daily deals could train users to wait. Mitigate: use additive inventory (flash/exclusive), not discounts on existing.
