# **Executive Summary**

This report provides a comprehensive analysis of how seven major competitors in the North American coupons, cashback and loyalty space are leveraging loyalty programmes to drive user engagement, retention and lifetime value. The findings are intended to inform Groupon’s strategic decision-making around the proposed Safari extension, Genie rewards, and cashback initiatives.

The competitive landscape is consolidating rapidly. Every major player now offers some form of cashback or rewards mechanism. The global cashback apps market was valued at approximately $8-10 billion in 2024 \[1\] \[2\] and is projected to reach $22-23 billion by 2032-33, growing at 9-12% CAGR. North America accounts for approximately 40% of global revenue. \[3\]

## **Key Findings at a Glance**

* All seven competitors analysed operate a loyalty or cashback programme; six of seven use browser extensions as a primary acquisition and engagement channel.

* Rakuten leads with 20M+ cumulative US signups since 1999 \[Estimate\] \[4\] and $4.6B in cumulative cashback paid since 1999 \[5\], and is now expanding into premium paid tiers (Rakuten+) \[6\] and points partnerships (Amex, Bilt). \[7\]

* PayPal Honey has lost over 8M Chrome users since its 2024 peak (\~21M to \~13M) following affiliate-fraud scandals \[8\] \[9\], creating a market share gap competitors are actively filling.

* Capital One Shopping has 10M+ users \[10\] and reportedly pays $40-80 in referral rewards to acquire new extension users (referral-gated, 90-day retention requirement; current Apr 2026 public offer $80/$80). \[11\]

* Ibotta has 54M+ registered users, 18.2M redeemers in 2025, and $342M FY2025 revenue \[12\]-proving that a cashback-first model can scale into enterprise.

* RetailMeNot reached 100M+ consumers across social platforms in 2025 and launched a Guaranteed Cash Back programme in Feb 2026 across 4,000+ retailers. \[13\] \[14\]

* Coupert has grown to 8M+ weekly active users \[self-reported Oct 2025, unaudited\] \[15\] with a combined coupon \+ cashback extension model, filling the gap left by Honey’s decline.

* Slickdeals is the \#2 ranked site in the US Coupons & Rebates category (behind Capital One Shopping) with 56.5M monthly visits and 12M MAU, driven by its community-powered deal-sharing model and 80% direct traffic share. \[62\]

The global cashback and rewards app market is experiencing robust growth. # **2\. Competitor Deep Dives**

## **2.1 Rakuten Rewards**

### **Overview**

Originally founded as Ebates in 1998 and acquired by Rakuten (Japan) in 2014 \[5\], Rakuten Rewards is the longest-running and most established cashback platform in North America. It operates across more than 3,500 partner stores \[4\] and has distributed over $4.6 billion in cumulative cashback rewards to its 20M+ cumulative US signups since 1999 \[Estimate \- Rakuten does not publish active-member counts\]. \[5\]

### **Platform & Channels**

Rakuten operates a full multi-channel ecosystem: a web shopping portal (rakuten.com), native mobile apps for iOS and Android, a browser extension available on Chrome, Safari, Firefox and Edge, and in-store cashback via linked payment cards. \[32\] The browser extension functions as a passive acquisition and engagement tool, surfacing cashback offers when users visit partner merchant websites.

### **Loyalty Mechanics & Currency**

Rakuten’s core currency is US dollar cashback, typically ranging from 1% to 20%+ depending on the merchant and promotional period. \[5\] In a significant strategic move, Rakuten now also supports earning Amex Membership Rewards points and Bilt Rewards points in place of cash, offering a 1:1 conversion ratio ($10 cashback \= 1,000 points). \[7\] In 2024, Rakuten launched Rakuten+, an invite-only paid membership programme targeting luxury shoppers with guaranteed 10% cashback from premium brands year-round. \[6\]

### **Terms, Conditions & Payout**

Payouts are processed quarterly on fixed dates (15 Feb, 15 May, 15 Aug, 15 Nov). Users need a minimum balance of $5.01 to trigger a payment. Cashback can be received via PayPal or physical cheque-gift cards are not an option. \[19\] Cashback does not expire while the account is active. The quarterly payout cycle creates a built-in retention mechanism as users maintain engagement to build their balance between payment periods. \[33\]

### **User Acquisition & Sign-Up Offers**

Rakuten currently runs its highest-ever referral bonus-$50 for both the referrer and the new member (extended through June 30, 2026). The standard non-referral welcome bonus is $10. To qualify, new members must make $50 in qualifying purchases within 90 days. \[20\] The $50 referral structure implies Rakuten values new user acquisition at at least $50 per head, suggesting an LTV well above that threshold. Rakuten’s core audience skews towards millennial women. \[6\]

### **Traffic & Scale**

Rakuten.com receives approximately 142M total monthly visits globally (Semrush, Sep 2025), though this includes the broader Rakuten ecosystem. \[34\] Desktop traffic is driven primarily by direct visits (64%) and organic search. The audience split is approximately 48% male, 52% female with the largest visitor segment aged 35-44. \[35\]

### **Strategic Implications for Groupon**

* Rakuten’s points partnerships (Amex, Bilt) are a masterclass in increasing perceived reward value without increasing cost. Groupon Bucks could explore similar partnerships.

* The Rakuten+ paid tier demonstrates willingness among high-value shoppers to pay for guaranteed elevated cashback. \[6\]

* Rakuten’s quarterly payout creates a natural engagement loop; Groupon Bucks with in-app-only redemption achieves a similar lock-in effect.

## **2.2 PayPal Honey**

### **Overview**

Founded in 2012 and acquired by PayPal in January 2020 for approximately $4 billion \[36\], Honey is the most high-profile browser extension in the coupons and cashback space. At its peak in late 2024, Honey had approximately 20-21 million Chrome Web Store users. \[8\] However, following a widely publicised investigation by YouTuber MegaLag exposing affiliate commission manipulation, Honey has experienced a dramatic decline-dropping to approximately 13 million Chrome users as of Dec 2025 (Apr 2026 live count pending direct verification), a loss of over 8 million users. \[9\]

### **Platform & Channels**

Honey operates primarily through its browser extension (Chrome, Safari, Firefox, Edge, Opera), a mobile app, and integration within the PayPal super-app. \[23\] Notably, Honey has already deployed a Safari extension for iOS-the exact model proposed in Groupon’s Safari initiative. When users shop in mobile Safari on iOS, Honey can surface coupons and cashback offers. \[37\] This validates the technical approach Groupon is considering.

### **Loyalty Mechanics & Currency**

Honey’s legacy rewards system (“Honey Gold”) has been migrated into PayPal Rewards. Users earn points when shopping at participating stores through the extension or PayPal app. Points are redeemable at $0.01 per point ($10 \= 1,000 points). Cash back can be redeemed as PayPal balance, gift cards, or donated to charity. \[22\] The integration with PayPal’s payment ecosystem provides a seamless redemption experience-however, this is only available to users with a PayPal account in good standing. \[21\]

### **Terms, Conditions & Payout**

Points can expire if the account is inactive. Not all purchases earn cashback-users must look for a trophy icon next to store names to identify participating merchants. \[22\] The 2024-2025 scandal revealed that Honey may not always surface the best available coupons, sometimes prioritising partner merchants. Google updated Chrome Web Store policies in March 2025 in direct response. \[38\]

### **User Acquisition & Scale**

Honey historically grew through massive influencer sponsorship campaigns (MrBeast, Linus Tech Tips, etc.). At its peak, Honey had \~17M monthly active users and claimed $1B+ in annual consumer savings. \[36\] The company does not currently offer a standard sign-up bonus. The ongoing user decline represents a significant market opportunity for competitors. A class action lawsuit was filed in December 2024, dismissed without prejudice in November 2025\. \[38\] A second round of allegations emerged in December 2025\. \[9\]

### **Strategic Implications for Groupon**

* Honey’s Safari extension on iOS validates the core technical approach for Groupon’s proposal. PayPal/Honey has proven App Store approval is achievable. \[37\]

* Honey’s dramatic user loss (\~8M+ on Chrome alone) creates a gap in the extension market. \[9\]

* The affiliate attribution scandal is a cautionary tale: transparency in how Groupon surfaces coupons will be critical to maintaining trust.

## **2.3 Capital One Shopping**

### **Overview**

Originally founded as Wikibuy in Austin, Texas in 2014, the platform was acquired by Capital One in 2018 and rebranded as Capital One Shopping in 2020\. \[25\] It has grown to serve over 10 million users \[10\] and maintains a 4.9/5 rating on the App Store (960,000+ reviews) and 4.8/5 on Google Play (64,000+ reviews). \[10\] Capital One Shopping does not require a Capital One credit card or bank account to use. \[39\]

### **Platform & Channels**

Capital One Shopping operates through a browser extension (Chrome, Firefox, Edge, Safari) and a mobile app. The extension automatically scans for coupon codes at checkout, compares prices across retailers (including shipping costs), and provides price history charts. \[39\] The price comparison capability differentiates Capital One Shopping from pure cashback platforms.

### **Loyalty Mechanics & Currency**

Unlike Rakuten or Ibotta, Capital One Shopping does not pay cash back. Instead, users earn “Shopping Credits” on qualifying purchases, which can only be redeemed for e-gift cards at approximately 50 participating retailers. \[24\] Credits cannot be converted to cash, statement credits, or transferred to loyalty programmes. Rewards are typically credited after the return window closes. \[24\]

### **User Acquisition & Cost**

Capital One Shopping’s most strategically significant data point is its reported willingness to pay $40-80 in referral rewards to new users who install the extension (referral-gated, 90-day retention requirement; current Apr 2026 public offer $80/$80). \[11\] This strongly validates that extension users carry high lifetime value-well above the stated referral cost. Capital One Shopping is also implicated in the same last-click attribution practices that led to scrutiny of Honey, with similar class action lawsuits filed in May 2025\. \[38\]

### **Strategic Implications for Groupon**

* The $40-80 referral benchmark provides strong evidence for the economics of browser extension users. \[11\] Groupon’s proposed Bucks-based activation incentive could achieve similar acquisition at lower CAC.

* Capital One Shopping’s gift-card-only redemption is a limitation-Groupon Bucks redeemable against local experiences offers a more compelling value proposition.

* The price comparison feature creates habitual extension engagement even when users aren’t actively coupon-hunting.

## **2.4 RetailMeNot**

### **Overview**

Founded in 2006 and now owned by Ziff Davis, RetailMeNot celebrated its 20th anniversary in January 2026\. Ziff Davis launched the RetailMeNot Group, unifying its US shopping portfolio including Offers.com, BlackFriday.com, TechBargains, and Deals of America. \[13\] RetailMeNot claims to have facilitated $1.7 billion in retailer sales and paid out $100+ million in cashback to consumers. \[13\] Its Android app has been downloaded over 19 million times. \[26\]

### **Platform & Channels**

RetailMeNot operates across web (retailmenot.com), native iOS/Android apps, and a mobile Safari browser extension. Organic search accounts for nearly 44% of desktop traffic, with direct visits at 46%. \[40\] RetailMeNot reached over 100 million consumers across TikTok, Instagram and other platforms in 2025\. \[13\] The mobile app features an “Automatic Stackable Savings” feature that combines codes and cashback offers. \[28\]

### **Loyalty Mechanics & Currency**

RetailMeNot operates a straightforward cashback model denominated in US dollars. In February 2026, RetailMeNot launched a Guaranteed Cash Back programme-guaranteeing a minimum 1% cashback across 4,000+ retailers, with some funded directly by RetailMeNot rather than the merchant. \[14\] Cashback is paid via PayPal or Venmo, typically within 45 days. \[27\] The company runs signature promotional events including National Cash Back Day (November), with rates up to 20-35% at select merchants. \[27\]

### **Strategic Implications for Groupon**

* RetailMeNot’s Guaranteed Cash Back is a bold competitive move-guaranteeing a floor of 1% even when merchant-funded rates are unavailable. \[14\]

* The parent company (Ziff Davis) experienced an 18% revenue decline in its Tech & Shopping segment, driven by weaker search traffic. \[41\] This signals vulnerability.

* RetailMeNot’s social-first strategy (100M+ reach) is a channel Groupon could learn from for driving extension installs and cashback programme awareness.

## **2.5 Ibotta**

### **Overview**

Founded in 2012 in Denver, Colorado, Ibotta went public in April 2024 and reported $342.4 million in revenue for FY2025 with $62.9 million in Adjusted EBITDA. Ibotta has over 54 million registered users, 18.2 million redeemers in 2025, and $2.7 billion in total cashback credited to consumers to date. \[12\]

### **Platform & Channels**

Ibotta operates through its consumer app (iOS/Android), a browser extension, and-critically-through its Ibotta Performance Network (IPN), which distributes digital offers through third-party publisher partners including Instacart, DoorDash, Walmart, Kroger and Dollar General. \[29\] The IPN had 17.3 million redeemers in Q2 2025 alone, up 27% year-over-year. \[42\]

### **Loyalty Mechanics & Currency**

Ibotta pays direct cashback in US dollars. Users earn rewards by scanning receipts, linking store loyalty accounts for automatic cashback, or shopping via the app/extension. Typical offers range from $0.25 to $5.00 per item. The minimum payout threshold is $20, redeemable via PayPal, Venmo, or gift cards. \[30\] Ibotta’s strength lies in CPG and grocery, where it provides brands with verified purchase data and performance-based promotional tools.

### **User Demographics & Acquisition**

Ibotta’s user base skews strongly female (72%) with the largest age segment being 25-54 (56% of users). Women account for 70% of the platform’s top savers. \[43\] The average user earns approximately $256 annually. \[44\] The company reported $320 million in revenue in 2023, up 52% from 2022\. \[45\] Ibotta was valued at $2.67 billion at IPO. \[29\]

### **Strategic Implications for Groupon**

* Ibotta’s B2B2C network model is the most strategically significant development in the space. Groupon could explore embedding Bucks earning into partner merchant checkout flows. \[29\]

* Ibotta’s grocery-heavy use case captures high-frequency everyday spending. Groupon’s local experiences are lower-frequency but higher-margin-the two are complementary.

* Ibotta’s strong financial performance ($342M revenue, profitable) demonstrates that cashback-first models can build substantial businesses. \[12\]

## **2.6 Coupert**

### **Overview**

Founded in 2017, Coupert reports more than 8 million weekly active users and partnerships with over 200,000 retailers worldwide. \[15\] Coupert powered Microsoft’s built-in coupon feature on Edge for four years (2021-2025) before Microsoft discontinued it in May 2025\. \[46\] Coupert holds a Chrome Web Store “Featured” badge and a 4.7-star rating across 10,800+ reviews. \[31\]

### **Platform & Channels**

Coupert operates through its browser extension (Chrome, Edge, Firefox, Safari) and a companion mobile app. The extension automatically tests and applies coupon codes at checkout, tracks price history, provides price-drop alerts, and offers integrated cashback. In independent testing, Coupert applied working coupons at approximately 73% of checkouts. \[47\]

### **Loyalty Mechanics & Currency**

Coupert uses a dual currency system: direct cashback and “Gold Coins” earned through various engagement activities. Users can cash out via PayPal, gift cards, or debit card with a remarkably low $1 minimum for the first withdrawal. \[31\] This low threshold reduces friction and increases the perception of immediate value. Coupert also offers a referral programme where users earn $20 for each new sign-up.

### **Strategic Implications for Groupon**

* Coupert’s growth demonstrates significant room for new entrants in the extension market, particularly as Honey loses users. \[15\]

* The low payout minimum ($1) is a competitive advantage-faster gratification drives habitual engagement.

* Coupert’s success as a Honey alternative post-scandal suggests that trust and transparency are now table-stakes differentiators.

## **2.7 Slickdeals**

### **Overview**

Founded in 1999 and headquartered in Las Vegas, Slickdeals is the largest community-driven deal-sharing platform in the United States. Unlike the other competitors profiled, Slickdeals’ core model is user-generated: deals are sourced, shared, voted on, and discussed by a community of 12 million monthly active users. \[61\] The platform claims to have saved shoppers over $10 billion over its 25+ year history and ranks \#2 in the US Coupons & Rebates category on SimilarWeb (behind Capital One Shopping) with 56.5M monthly visits. \[62\]

Scope note: Slickdeals is included here as a loyalty and audience-overlap reference point. A separate partnership workstream (see Appendix 8.4) evaluates a potential Groupon-Slickdeals embedded-feed deal and is out of scope for this report.

### **Platform & Channels**

Slickdeals operates through its website (slickdeals.net), native iOS and Android apps, a browser extension for Chrome, Firefox, and Edge, and a Cashback Rewards programme. The website is the primary platform, with notably strong engagement metrics: 4.05 pages per visit, 4:50 average session duration, and a 37.3% bounce rate \[62\]-all significantly better than any other competitor profiled. This reflects the community-forum nature of the site, where users browse, read discussions, and explore multiple deals per session. Slickdeals also operates a Deal Alerts system that sends notifications when user-specified products or categories have new deals.

### **Loyalty Mechanics & Currency**

Slickdeals launched its Cashback Rewards programme, allowing members to earn points on qualifying purchases that can be redeemed for PayPal credit or gift cards. \[63\] The programme has paid out over $1 million in cashback to date. This is a smaller-scale programme compared to Rakuten or Ibotta, reflecting Slickdeals’ heritage as a deal discovery and community platform rather than a cashback-first model. Slickdeals explicitly advises users to disable other cashback extensions when using its programme, as multiple extensions can conflict. \[64\]

### **Traffic & Scale**

Slickdeals receives 56.5M monthly visits (March 2026), ranking it \#2 in the US Coupons & Rebates category in the US (behind Capital One Shopping). \[62\] Traffic is overwhelmingly direct (approximately 80%), indicating extremely high brand loyalty and habitual usage. \[65\] The company reports $25-50M annual revenue with 51-200 employees. The platform generates over 1 billion page views annually across web and app. \[61\]

### **Strategic Implications for Groupon**

* Slickdeals ranks \#6 on Groupon’s SimilarWeb audience affinity data (0.848 affinity score)-confirming strong audience overlap with Groupon’s users.

* Slickdeals’ community-driven model creates exceptionally sticky engagement (4:50 avg session, 4+ pages/visit). Groupon should study this as a benchmark for what “habitual usage” looks like in the deals/coupons space.

* Slickdeals’ cashback offering is still nascent ($1M paid out vs. Rakuten’s $4.6B), suggesting the community-first model is strong but cashback is not its core competency-this is a gap Groupon could exploit.

* Slickdeals’ 80% direct traffic share is the highest in the competitive set, demonstrating that a strong community/brand can drive habitual visits without reliance on search. Groupon’s Bucks \+ local experiences could create a similar loyalty loop.

## **4.4 The Switching vs. Stacking Question**

A critical strategic question is whether Groupon needs users to *switch* from an existing cashback platform, or whether users can be encouraged to *stack* Groupon alongside their current tools. The evidence strongly suggests that stacking is the dominant consumer behaviour in this category:

* Multiple industry guides explicitly recommend installing 2-3 extensions simultaneously. The WalletGrower 2026 guide recommends “Install Rakuten first, add Honey for coupon coverage, layer in Capital One Shopping for price protection.” \[39\] This means consumers are already habituated to multi-tool usage.

* The SimilarWeb channel data supports this: Capital One Shopping’s 81% direct traffic and 7.8% referral share (5.2M visits) suggest users arrive via extension triggers and cross-platform referrals-not by searching “cashback apps” and choosing one. They use whatever tool activates at checkout. \[35\]

* Honey’s 14.6% referral traffic (1.3M visits/month from PayPal and related services) demonstrates ecosystem cross-pollination rather than standalone loyalty. \[35\]

* Groupon’s unique advantage is that Bucks are redeemable against local experiences-something no cashback competitor offers. This means Groupon does not need to compete on cashback rates (where Rakuten and Coupert will always have scale advantages). Instead, the value proposition is additive: “Earn savings from Nike via your existing tools, plus earn Groupon Bucks that fund a spa day near you.”

The implication is that the primary challenge is not switching users away from competitors-it is making Groupon visible and valuable enough to earn a place in the user’s existing savings stack. The Safari extension bundled inside the Groupon app is perfectly positioned for this: zero friction install, no competing extension to displace, and a unique reward currency.

## **4.5 Recommended Research Programme**

To move from directional evidence to investment-grade confidence, the following research programme is recommended. These studies are ordered by speed and cost, with the fastest and cheapest first.

### **Phase 1: Desk Research (1-2 weeks, minimal cost)**

**Keyword Overlap via Semrush or Ahrefs:** Run a keyword gap analysis comparing groupon.com against all seven competitors for coupon, cashback, and deal-related search terms. This reveals which competitor audiences Groupon is already capturing via organic search and where the gaps are-providing a proxy for user overlap at the search-intent level.

### **Phase 2: Quantitative User Survey (3-4 weeks, \~$15-25K)**

Commission a survey of 1,000-2,000 Groupon coupons page visitors (intercept survey via Qualtrics or Hotjar) to understand:

* Current cashback tool usage: Which platforms do they already use? (Rakuten, Honey, Capital One Shopping, Ibotta, Coupert, none)

* Frequency and satisfaction: How often do they use their current tool? How satisfied are they? What frustrates them?

* Willingness to add Groupon: Would they install a Groupon Safari extension / Genie extension alongside their current tools? What would motivate them? (Bucks, cashback rate, ease of use, exclusive offers)

* Bucks vs. cash preference: Conjoint analysis testing the perceived value of $5 Groupon Bucks (redeemable on local experiences) vs. $5 cashback (via PayPal) vs. $5 gift card. This directly tests whether the Bucks value proposition resonates or whether users discount non-cash rewards.

* Switching triggers: What would make them stop using their current cashback tool? (Better rates, trust issues, simpler UX, unique rewards)

* Local experience interest: Do they currently book local experiences (restaurants, spas, activities)? Would earning rewards on online shopping that they could spend on local experiences appeal to them?

### **Phase 3: Behavioural Analysis of Existing Data (2-3 weeks, internal)**

* Genie install-to-purchase analysis: For the existing 981 Genie installs, analyse what percentage of users also have accounts on Groupon’s core marketplace. This provides the first directional data on whether extension users convert into deal buyers.

* Coupons-to-deals cross-purchase analysis: Using Groupon’s existing user database, identify how many coupons page visitors (by cookie/session) have ever purchased a Groupon deal. This quantifies the current organic crossover rate-the baseline that cashback and Bucks would need to improve upon.

* Bloomreach email cohort test: If registration infrastructure is in place, run a Bloomreach-powered email test offering Groupon Bucks to registered coupons users who have never purchased a deal. Measure conversion rate and Bucks redemption rate to validate the Bucks-as-bridge hypothesis.

### **Phase 4: Qualitative Depth Interviews (4-6 weeks, \~$10-20K)**

Recruit 15-20 heavy cashback users (defined as using 2+ cashback platforms and earning $100+/year in rewards) for 45-minute video interviews. The interview guide should explore:

* Their mental model of loyalty: Do they feel “loyal” to Rakuten or Honey, or are they loyal to the behaviour of seeking savings regardless of platform?

* Extension fatigue: How many browser extensions are too many? Would they install a Groupon extension if it offered something their current tools do not?

* The Bucks concept test: Present the concept of earning rewards on Nike.com that can be spent on a massage near them. Gauge emotional response, perceived value, and likelihood of adoption.

* The Safari extension concept test: Present the in-app Safari extension concept. Does bundling inside the Groupon app feel more trustworthy than a standalone extension install? (Post-Honey-scandal, trust is a significant variable.)

* Competitive displacement mapping: For each platform they currently use, ask what would need to be true for them to replace it with Groupon. Identify which competitor is most vulnerable to displacement and why.

### **Phase 5: Live A/B Testing (Ongoing, part of PoC)**

Once the Safari extension or Genie rewards are in MVP, build measurement into the product itself:

* Extension activation A/B test: Test different onboarding flows and incentive levels ($5 Bucks vs. $10 Bucks vs. no incentive) on Safari extension activation rate.

* Stacking behaviour tracking: Monitor whether users who install the Groupon extension retain their existing Rakuten or Honey extensions, or uninstall them. This is measurable via browser extension detection at checkout.

* Bucks redemption funnel: Track from Bucks-earned to Bucks-redeemed to deal-purchased. The conversion rate at each stage will determine the true unit economics of the cashback-to-deals flywheel.

# **6\. Implications for Groupon’s Strategy**

## **6.1 Groupon’s Unique Position**

Unlike every competitor analysed, Groupon is the only platform that can bridge online coupons to local, in-person experiences. Rakuten bridges coupons to retail rewards. Ibotta bridges receipts to cashback. But only Groupon can offer “Earn Bucks from Nike, spend them on a massage near you.” This unique cross-sell between online savings and local experiences is an unmatched strategic moat-but only if the infrastructure to capture off-platform activity is built.

## **6.2 Validation of Proposed Initiatives**

**This competitive research strongly validates each layer of the proposed coupons strategy:**

**Layer 1 - Monetise:** Every competitor is monetising coupon traffic through multiple channels. Groupon’s 30M annual sessions represent a clear under-monetised asset.

**Layer 2 - Genie \+ Rewards:** Extension users are demonstrably the highest-value segment. Capital One’s $40-80 referral-gated acquisition cost (90-day retention) \[11\], Rakuten’s $50 referral bonus \[20\], and Coupert’s rapid growth \[15\] all confirm the economics. Honey’s scandal has created a unique acquisition window. \[9\]

**Layer 3 - Safari Extension:** Honey and Rakuten both operate iOS Safari extensions today, confirming App Store feasibility. \[37\] With 84% of Groupon app users on iOS, this is the single largest untapped engagement channel.

**Cashback:** Every major competitor now operates a cashback programme. The market is projected to nearly triple by 2033\. \[1\] Not having a cashback offering will increasingly put Groupon at a competitive disadvantage.

## **6.3 Risks & Considerations**

* Regulatory scrutiny of extensions: The Honey scandal led to Chrome Web Store policy changes in March 2025 and multiple class action lawsuits. \[38\] Any extension-based strategy must be designed with transparent affiliate practices from the outset.

* User fatigue: Consumers may resist installing another extension. Bundling Safari extension inside the existing Groupon app (as proposed) is a significant advantage-no separate install required.

* Payout economics: Competitors paying cash face ongoing cost pressure. Groupon Bucks, redeemable only in-app against deals, offer a structurally lower cost-of-reward while driving the core business.

* Competitive response: If Groupon’s Safari extension gains traction, expect Rakuten and Honey to pursue deeper app integrations. Speed of execution matters.

* Cannibalisation risk: Bucks issued on off-Groupon purchases may substitute for, rather than add to, Bucks issued on Groupon deals. Net margin impact depends on the incrementality rate, which is currently unmeasured. See Section 6.5 Data to Pull item 4 (coupons to Groupon crossover rate).

## **6.4 Recommended Prioritisation**

Based on this competitive analysis, the following prioritisation aligns with market dynamics:

1. **Immediate (Now): Layer 1 monetisation (display ads, deal cross-sell, Genie CTA) - \~8 MD. Zero dependencies. Every competitor already does this.**  
2. **High conviction (Q2): Genie rewards integration - \~15 MD. Honey’s user loss creates urgency. Cross-channel data shows \+110% revenue uplift for extension users with rewards.**  
3. **Strategic bet (Q2-Q3): Safari extension 2-week spike \+ Registration. The 84% iOS user base, combined with validated models from Honey and Rakuten, makes this the highest-leverage initiative for FY2026.**  
4. **Growth driver (Q3+): Cashback PoC. Every competitor has cashback. Groupon’s Bucks-based model is structurally advantaged but requires the identity/registration layer first.**

The model breaks into three layers (per Rebecca's 2026-04-23 proposal):

### **Layer 1 \- Acquisition**

Three independent acquisition rows, each producing a registered/active-user count:

* Safari extension installs: share of iOS Groupon app users who enable the extension (reference: Honey-via-PayPal-app bundle; Capital One Shopping Top Free Shopping iOS rank).

### **Layer 2 \- Usage / Impressions**

Easy for coupons pages, hard for Safari:

* Coupons pages impressions: use the 4,000-brand RetailMeNot coverage list as baseline; apply existing coupons page impression volume and estimate click-through to cashback CTA.  
* Safari extension impressions: this is the hardest input. Assume an installed user visits the 4,000 retailer sites N times/year. N is unknown externally. Approach: instrument Genie on top 5 high-traffic merchants with a reward offer, back-calculate per-user impression rate, extrapolate to Safari install base x 4,000 sites. Genie-as-testbed is the only internal proxy we have.  
* Genie usage: existing Genie engagement metrics with Rewards overlay.

Key metric out: impressions\_by\_channel\_per\_year.

### **Layer 3 \- Claim conversion, breakage, ROI**

Depends on functional decisions made in the mechanic cards:

* Claim conversion rate: % of impressions \-\> claim \-\> validated purchase. Industry benchmarks via Rakuten / Ibotta / Capital One Shopping.  
* Cashback rate (% of purchase paid back): lever, managed as a program; model as variable with default 1-2%.  
* Commission uplift: cashback programs tend to earn slightly higher commission than pure coupons (+0.5pp vs coupons baseline).  
* Breakage rate (% of earned Bucks never redeemed): 15-30% industry. Gift-card-only adds 3-10% compounding.  
* Bucks-for-engagement cost: if small Bucks amounts are awarded for app opens / push notification clicks, this is a small fixed OpEx per active user.

Key metric out: net\_annual\_revenue\_uplift by scenario.

### **Mechanic Card 01 \- Quarterly Payout Ritual**

Source: Rakuten "Big Fat Check" | 2026-04-21

What it is

Four fixed payout dates per year where accumulated cashback lands as a visible, anticipated payment. Rakuten pays on Feb 15, May 15, Aug 15, Nov 15 \- always the 15th. Methods: cheque, PayPal, Amex MR points (1:100), Bilt points. Minimum threshold: $5.01. \[Data \- rakuten.com/blog; High\]

Why it works as a habit driver

\* Dopamine anticipation 4x/year. Users check back approaching each date to see their balance.

\* Brand ritual. "Big Fat Check" has its own name, schedule, FAQ page, marketing moments. It becomes a thing users talk about.

\* Soft lock drives breakage. Sub-threshold balances ($5.01 min) roll forward. Many users forget or abandon. Unredeemed cashback \= margin.

\* Forces sub-quarterly engagement. Users visit to accrue toward the next payout.

Evidence this works

\* Rakuten's rakuten.com has 63% direct traffic \[Data \- SimilarWeb Mar 2026\] \- users bookmark / type in. Strong repeat usage.

\* 20M+ cumulative US signups since 1999 \[Estimate\]; $4.6B cumulative cashback paid since 1999 \[Data\]

\* The mechanic has survived 27 years unchanged. That is the strongest survival signal in the category.

Groupon adoption

Copy the ritual. Skip the anti-customer details.

\* Quarterly cadence: pay on Feb 15 / May 15 / Aug 15 / Nov 15 (or Groupon-branded alternative \- e.g. "Bucks Drop Day" with its own monthly-15th anchor).

\* Payout methods: PayPal, Venmo, Groupon credit, OR cash-out to linked debit card at $1 minimum (Coupert-style, see card 04).

\* Brand the ritual. Give it a name, a page, a push notification campaign, a homepage takeover.

\* Cumulative earnings counter prominently on the home screen ("You've earned $127.43 this year; next payout Aug 15").

Specifically do NOT copy

\* $5.01 minimum payout threshold \- anti-customer. Use $1 instead (per Coupert). Accept the small UX cost; trade for trust.

\* Pending-balance denials \- Rakuten aggressively invalidates pending balances. For Groupon, reserve-and-release, never confirm-and-revoke.

\* Bounced cheques \- PhatWallet reports of Big Fat Check bouncing \[Data \- PhatWallet Mar 2024\]. If Groupon pays, it pays.

Effort

\* Backend ledger: \~1 sprint to build accumulating Bucks ledger per user.

\* Cadence \+ payout job: \~0.5 sprint.

\* Marketing / brand: \~1 sprint for the first "Bucks Drop Day" campaign.

\* Cumulative counter UX: \~0.5 sprint.

Total: \~2-3 engineering sprints \+ design \+ marketing.

Success metrics

\* % of users with a positive Bucks balance on payout day

\* Push open rate on "Bucks Drop" notification

\* Visits-per-user in the 14 days prior to payout date (should spike)

\* Payout redemption rate (target \>70%; anything \<50% means either UX friction or distrust)

\* Breakage rate (aim for 15-25% steady state \- above 30% means users are losing trust)

Risks

\* If Groupon cannot fund the cashback (Rakuten's 2.4% op margin is the category ceiling), the ritual reveals weak economics. Pilot at small scale before scaling.

\* If Groupon cuts the rate after launch, the ritual amplifies the damage (rate cuts on established loyalty programmes are a well-documented backlash trigger across categories). Commit to 24 months of stable rate minimum before ever adjusting.

### **Mechanic Card 02 \- 1:1 Transparent Soft Currency (Groupon Bucks)**

Source: Capital One Shopping Credits | 2026-04-21

What it is

A branded soft currency where 1 unit \= 1 dollar of redemption value, with NO obfuscation. Users earn "Bucks" per purchase. Bucks redeem against Groupon deals, gift cards, or select partner retailers. Clarity is the load-bearing design choice.

Capital One Shopping's implementation: 1 credit \= $1 USD (transparent), gift-card-only redemption at 50+ rotating retailers, no cash-out, no bank deposit, no statement credit, no Capital One banking account required. \[Data \- Capital One help center; High\]

Why 1:1 beats points systems

\* Clarity \= trust. Every time a user sees "1,000 points \= ???", they have to do math. Honey does this (100 points \= $1.00). Rakuten partially does this (cashback in $ but Amex transfer at 1:100). Capital One Shopping's 1:1 is the clean variant.

\* No devaluation gaming. Points programs allow issuer to secretly devalue by changing redemption rates. High-profile examples across multiple loyalty categories have triggered customer backlash when long-held earn rates are silently cut. A 1:1 ratio makes devaluation impossible without a visible change users will notice.

\* Balance sheet elegance. Bucks are a liability on the books, not a cash outflow. Breakage converts liability to profit.

Breakage economics

\* Industry benchmark: 15-30% of earned rewards never redeem \[Estimate \- multiple sources\]

\* Gift-card-only redemption adds a second layer of breakage (3-10% industry) \[Estimate\]

\* Compounding: issue Bucks \-\> \~25% never redeemed \-\> of redeemed, \~5-10% of gift card value never spent \-\> real cash cost to issuer is 60-80% of headline "earned" \[Opinion\]

For Groupon, breakage on Bucks could plausibly be the difference between 2% and 8% margin on the loyalty program. Pilot before scaling.

### **Mechanic Card 03 \- Weekly Anchor Day**

Source: Rakuten Mobile Monday | 2026-04-21

What it is

A weekly recurring event on a specific weekday that rewards opening the app / extension / website that day.

Rakuten Mobile Monday: every Monday, in-app only, boosted cashback rates. Drives weekly app open. \[Data \- rakuten.com/double-cash-back; High\] Rakuten has maintained this mechanic for multiple years, making it one of the longest-running weekly anchors in the cashback category.

Why it works as a habit driver

\* Deterministic weekly hook. Users learn "Monday \= Groupon day" over 4-6 weeks of consistent pattern.

\* Low effort to participate. Opening an app is trivial compared to purchasing.

\* Fills gap between quarterly payouts. Without a sub-quarterly mechanic, the Big Fat Check cycle is 13 weeks between dopamine moments. Weekly anchor compresses this to 1 week.

\* Creates pushing surface. Every Sunday night / Monday morning is a legitimate push notification opportunity without feeling spammy.

Evidence this works

\* Rakuten direct traffic 63% \[Data \- SimilarWeb Mar 2026\]. Suggests strong habit-formation.

\* Rakuten has maintained Mobile Monday as a standing program for multiple years. Survival \= evidence.

\* Category pattern: committed weekly anchors are present across multiple loyalty categories, not just cashback. The mechanic is generic and well-validated.

### **Mechanic Card 04 \- Safari Extension Bundled With Groupon App**

Source: PayPal Honey via PayPal iOS app \- plus trust-positive variant | 2026-04-21

What it is

The Groupon iOS app ships with a Safari Web Extension pre-installed. Users do not separately install the extension \- it arrives silently during a regular Groupon app update. Activation requires one user action inside the Groupon app: tap "Deals" tab \-\> tap "Enable Safari extension" \-\> grant the iOS permission prompt. Once enabled, the Groupon extension surfaces at retailer checkouts automatically.

Honey's implementation inside the PayPal app is the reference \[Data \- help.joinhoney.com, paypal.com/us/cshelp/article/593; High\]. Works in 10 countries (US, CA, UK, AU, DE, FR, NL, IT, ES, IN). Capital One Shopping also does this with Safari extension \#2 Top Free Shopping app ranking \[Data \- Apple Community July 2025; Medium\].

Why it works

\* Zero-friction distribution. Users don't install Groupon's extension deliberately \- they install the Groupon app deliberately, and the extension comes with it.

\* Uses Apple's permission model. iOS Safari Web Extensions are a native Apple product since iOS 15\. Distribution via parent app is an Apple-sanctioned pattern.

\* Insulated from Chrome-style exodus. Chrome users can uninstall a browser extension in one click. Safari iOS extensions are buried in Settings, and users who got them via app bundling often don't know they have them. This is why Honey's Chrome user count fell from 20M to 12M in 12 months while iOS user count has not visibly cratered \[Opinion \- high confidence, public tracking asymmetry\].

\* Leverages Groupon's existing 30M+ app installs with 84% iOS share. \[Data \- Groupon internal; High\] That's \~25M iOS installs \= addressable activation pool.

\* Defensible moat. Once activated, the extension is always there at checkout. It becomes part of the user's shopping infrastructure, not a website they choose to visit.

Critical design choice: the trust-positive variant

Honey's version of this has a bundle-lock: users cannot uninstall Honey independently of the PayPal wallet. They must uninstall PayPal entirely. German press (heise.de) has flagged this as a dark pattern, and it has been the subject of multiple Apple Community complaints. \[Data \- heise.de, discussions.apple.com; High\]

Groupon must build the trust-positive variant: Safari extension bundled by default, but independently removable. Add a "Remove Safari extension" button inside Groupon app settings. This: \- Avoids heise.de-style dark-pattern criticism \- Protects Groupon from possible future App Store policy changes on tying \- Is actually a marketing asset ("unlike Honey, we let you remove it") \- Costs Groupon almost nothing \- users who uninstall the extension were unlikely to use it anyway

Activation rate assumption

Publicly undisclosed by Honey, Rakuten, and Capital One Shopping. Groupon sizing should assume 10-30% of app users activate until we have our own data. \[Estimate, Low confidence\]

\* Lower bound (10%): 2.5M active Safari extension users from 25M iOS installs.

\* Upper bound (30%): 7.5M active Safari extension users.

\* Middle (20%): 5M active Safari extension users.

Even the lower bound is larger than Genie's current \~981 installs. The order-of-magnitude difference is what makes this worth the investment.

Rollout sequence

Phase 1 \- Minimum viable MVP (6-8 weeks): \- US only \- 3-5 launch merchants (e.g. major retailers with existing Groupon coupon deals) \- Extension does ONE thing: auto-apply best coupon code at checkout, with on-screen confirmation "Groupon applied \[code\]" \- Optional: report to user whether they earned Bucks on the purchase \- NO silent cookie overwrites, NO private-code scraping, NO merchant-paid code suppression (per Honey class action)

Phase 2 (Q3): \- Expand merchant list to 500+ \- Add Bucks earn surface in the extension \- Add a "Groupon is earning affiliate commission from this purchase; we pay it back as Bucks" disclosure banner (the trust-positive version of Honey's attribution hijacking)

Phase 3 (Q4+): \- Country expansion (CA, UK initially; match Honey's 10-country footprint over 12-18 months) \- Price comparison alerts (Capital One Shopping's universal-credit hook, Card 05\) \- Droplist / watchlist (Honey's best uncontroversial feature)

Specifically do NOT copy (from Honey)

\* Silent cookie overwrites / last-click hijack at checkout. This is the criminal mechanic in the Honey class action. Groupon extension must NEVER overwrite an affiliate cookie it did not originate.

\* Merchant-paid coupon suppression. If Groupon has a merchant agreement that restricts code visibility, disclose it inline at the point of use.

\* Scraping user-typed private codes. MegaLag Part 2 allegation. Design the code database to accept only codes from verified merchant feeds or affiliate network feeds.

\* Bundle-lock (cannot uninstall extension without uninstalling app). Per above \- ship with independent removal.

\* Aggressive creator sponsorships as primary acquisition channel. YouTube creators are now Honey's class-action plaintiffs. Diversify before scaling.

Merchant coverage strategy

Honey's vulnerability in MegaLag Part 2: 181,000 stores listed in the extension, only 35,000 with actual affiliate partnerships. The rest were listed without merchant consent. This is structurally fragile and legally exposed.

Groupon's better model: only list merchants Groupon has active affiliate or direct relationships with. Groupon's network is narrower than Honey's (by design) but fully contracted. Lean into this as a trust differentiator: "every merchant in the Groupon extension has a legitimate affiliate or direct relationship with Groupon."

Effort

\* iOS Safari Web Extension build: 6-8 weeks (mobile eng team, first time through)

\* Groupon app integration \+ activation prompt UI: \~1 sprint

\* Attribution \+ Bucks earn plumbing: \~1 sprint (reuses Bucks ledger from Card 02\)

\* App Store resubmission \+ review cycle: 2-4 weeks

\* Launch merchants coupon sync: \~2 sprints

Total: \~10-14 weeks to US MVP.

Success metrics

\* Install → activate conversion in first 30 days after app update (target: 15-25% Phase 1\)

\* Monthly active extension users (ratio vs app MAU)

\* % of retailer checkouts where extension surfaces a code (target: \>60%)

\* Extension-assisted conversion rate lift (extension users vs non-extension users)

\* Bucks earned per extension user per month

\* Uninstall rate after first 30 days (target: \<15%; if higher, UX is broken)

Risks

\* App Store policy evolution. Apple could restrict Safari extensions in parent app bundles at any time. Mitigate by offering standalone extension as well.

\* EU Digital Markets Act. Possible tying concerns. Mitigate with independent-removal button.

\* Merchant pushback. If extension is seen as hijacking, merchants cut affiliate deals (as they did with Honey). Mitigate with clear attribution, no overwrites, public policy on code sourcing.

\* Registration / identity dependency. Extension needs to know who the user is to credit Bucks. Anonymous coupons users must be onboarded into the loyalty identity layer first. This is the critical blocker for Q2.

### **Mechanic Card 05 \- Trust Floor (Guaranteed Minimum \+ $1 Cashout)**

Sources: RetailMeNot 1% guaranteed floor (Feb 25, 2026\) \+ Coupert $1 withdrawal | 2026-04-21

What it is

Two complementary trust primitives:

1\. Guaranteed minimum rate. Every qualifying purchase through Groupon earns AT LEAST X% back, regardless of merchant-specific rate. RetailMeNot's version: 1% floor across 4,000+ retailers launched Feb 25 2026 \[Data \- retailmenot.mediaroom.com; High\].

2\. $1 cashout floor. Users can withdraw any balance over $1 (with a small processing fee). Coupert's version: $1 min via Visa/Mastercard with 2% \+ $0.50 fee \[Data \- Coupert help center; High\].

Together these reframe cashback from "a future promise I may never collect" to "real money I could take out tomorrow if I wanted."

Why each works

The guaranteed floor

\* Removes shopping-around from the decision tree. If every purchase earns at least 1%, users stop comparing rates across tools. Groupon becomes the default.

\* Turns variable rates into brand certainty. Users don't need to know what each merchant pays; they know the minimum.

\* Works as a trust primitive, not a loyalty mechanic per se. It's not designed to drive habit; it's designed to make the app trustable.

The $1 cashout floor

\* Validates the system works on the first small purchase. A new user earns 47 cents, cashes it out, gets a Venmo notification \- the trust is cemented immediately.

\* Breaks the "abandoned balance" anti-pattern that haunts Honey (1,000 points min \= $10), Rakuten ($5.01 min), and Capital One Shopping (gift-card-only, \~$5+ values). Those thresholds silently favour the issuer.

\* Counter-intuitively, aligning with user incentive builds more habit. Users who have cashed out once are more likely to come back than users who still haven't hit the threshold to redeem.

Why both together

Individually, either is thin. Combined they become: "You will earn at least X% back on every purchase, and you can pull it out as real money for $1." That is a rational-default framing that no competitor currently has.

Evidence

\* RetailMeNot launched the 1% floor as a defensive move amid 18% segment revenue decline \[Data \- Ziff Davis Q4 2025 earnings Feb 2026\]. The fact that they chose THIS as the differentiator suggests it is the mechanic that most resists AI-search displacement.

\* Coupert's $1 floor appears prominently in every review and comparison of the tool. It is the one mechanic users mention unprompted.

\* Both mechanics are LOW engineering cost and LOW margin impact at small scale. The value is brand/positioning, not unit economics.

Groupon adoption

Copy both, integrate with Bucks:

\* Floor: every qualifying Bucks-earning purchase earns minimum 1 Buck per $100 spent, even if the merchant pays \<1% commission. Groupon makes up the difference. \[Cost: negligible at pilot scale; needs finance model at full scale\]

\* Cashout: any Bucks balance \>$1 can be withdrawn to PayPal, Venmo, or linked debit card. Processing fee: 2% \+ $0.50 (matches Coupert). \[Small processing cost, offset by trust value\]

Communication framing

Don't bury this in FAQ. Put it on the homepage and extension UX:

\* "Every purchase earns at least 1% back. Guaranteed."

\* "Cash out any Bucks balance above $1 \- your money, anytime."

Specifically do NOT copy

\* Hidden floor caveats. If the floor has exceptions, disclose them up front. RetailMeNot's fine print excludes some categories; any Groupon version must be clearly enumerated.

\* Cashout fees hidden in T\&Cs. If the fee is 2% \+ $0.50, say so at the withdrawal confirmation screen. Don't let users be surprised.

\* Minimum thresholds disguised as floors. Rakuten's $5.01 is called a "minimum payout threshold", which is a soft lock. Coupert's $1 is called a "minimum withdrawal", which is a genuine escape valve. Language matters.

Interaction with other mechanics

\* Quarterly payout ritual (Card 01): The floor sets the baseline; quarterly payouts are the ceremony. Users know they'll always get something each quarter because of the floor.

\* Soft currency 1:1 (Card 02): Floor applies to Bucks earning rate; cashout is an alternative redemption path alongside Groupon-deal and gift-card redemption.

\* Safari extension (Card 04): Floor is the headline feature on the extension's post-checkout "you earned Bucks" screen.

Effort

\* Floor implementation: \~0.5 sprint (merchant config logic \- floor applies if merchant-specific rate \< 1%)

\* $1 cashout: \~1-2 sprints (payment-rail integration; partnership or payment processor required)

\* T\&Cs \+ disclosure UX: \~1 week legal \+ design

Total: \~2-3 sprints.

Success metrics

\* % of purchases where the floor boosts the base rate (tells us how often it fires; lower is fine \- it's a safety net)

\* First-month cashout rate for new users (target: 20-40% cash out at least once in first 30 days \- proves the mechanic is trusted)

\* Avg cashout size (Coupert reports users cash out \~$5-10 on average; smaller avg is better trust signal than larger)

\* User-reported trust metrics in survey data (NPS on "I trust I will get paid" category)

\* Cashout rate higher than expected. If users cash out aggressively, Groupon loses the redeem-against-deals flywheel. Monitor closely; if \>40% of Bucks are cashed out vs spent on Groupon deals, the mechanic needs revisiting.

\* Regulatory. Payment processor rules, state money-transmitter laws. Legal review. Likely handled via existing Groupon payment infrastructure if PayPal/Venmo are rails.

### **Mechanic Card 06 \- Trust and Transparency (The Anti-Honey Checklist)**

Source: Forensic reverse engineering of PayPal Honey 2024-2026 trust collapse | 2026-04-21

What it is

Not a single mechanic. A design discipline that governs every other mechanic. The discipline is simple: every loyalty mechanic must be transparently user-positive. If a mechanic only works when users don't understand it, it is a liability waiting for a viral expose.

This card exists because PayPal Honey lost 40% of Chrome users (20M \-\> 12M) in 12 months after a YouTube video explained how Honey actually worked. Three major affiliate networks cut Honey off over five days in Jan 2026\. A 101-page class action is active. The failure mode is specific, documented, and entirely avoidable.

The Honey failure-mode catalog

From MegaLag Part 1 (Dec 2024), Part 2 (Dec 2025), class action complaint, affiliate network statements:

1\. Silent cookie overwrites at checkout. Honey's popup at checkout would overwrite the creator's affiliate cookie with Honey's. User got "no better code found" but the click hijacked the commission. MegaLag's smoking gun: $35 NordVPN commission rerouted from a creator to Honey while user got $0.89 Honey Gold back.

2\. Merchant-paid code suppression. Honey had contractual agreements with some merchants to NOT surface codes better than a merchant-approved default. Users saw "no better code found" when RetailMeNot had obviously better codes one click away.

3\. Private-code scraping (MegaLag Part 2). Honey scraped codes users manually typed (employee codes, refund codes, retention-offer codes) and rebroadcast them to all Honey users. Merchants asked for takedown; Honey refused unless they became paying partners.

4\. Bundle-lock. Honey iOS extension cannot be uninstalled independently of the PayPal wallet app. Users who want to remove Honey must remove PayPal.

5\. Long pending windows (60-90 days) combined with 365-day inactivity forfeiture, combined with $10 minimum redemption threshold, combined with account-closure forfeiture of all points. Each looks reasonable individually; together they form a breakage-optimisation machine.

6\. "Not your property, not money, no value prior to redemption" T\&C language. Standard for points programs, but this language becomes the legal team's nightmare in a class action.

7\. Creator sponsorships as primary acquisition channel. YouTube creators, once the marketing engine, became the named plaintiffs.

8\. PayPal's defence ("industry standard", "legacy code"). This framing made the situation worse \- it conceded the practices while shifting blame backwards. Never rely on "everyone does it" as defence.

The Groupon checklist

Every mechanic card in this research has a "specifically do NOT copy" section derived from this failure-mode catalog. Consolidating:

Design principles (non-negotiable)

1\. No silent cookie overwrites. Groupon's extension must NEVER overwrite an affiliate cookie it did not originate.

2\. No merchant-paid code suppression. If Groupon has merchant agreements that restrict code visibility, disclose them inline at the point of use.

3\. No private-code scraping. Code database accepts only codes from verified merchant feeds or affiliate network feeds.

4\. Independent uninstall. Groupon's Safari extension must be removable from Groupon app settings without uninstalling the Groupon app.

5\. Short pending windows. 7-15 days max (matching Coupert), not 60-90 (Honey).

6\. No silent expiration. Any Buck expiration requires 30/60/90-day warning email cadence and a clear in-app countdown.

7\. Account closure does not forfeit earned Bucks. Closed accounts get a final redemption window.

8\. $1 cashout floor (per Card 05). No artificial minimum thresholds disguised as "minimums."

9\. Legal T\&Cs in plain language. No "not your property, not money, no value" phrasing. Every sentence should read cleanly in front of a jury.

10\. Diverse acquisition channels from day one. Don't concentrate on creator sponsorships. Paid social, app-store organic, partnerships, referral, content \- broad mix.

Proactive disclosure UX

Things Groupon should build because Honey didn't:

\* Attribution banner in Safari extension. "Groupon is earning affiliate commission on this purchase. We pay it back to you as Bucks." Shown once per merchant per session, not on every transaction.

\* Code provenance display. When the extension applies a code, show where the code came from: "Code verified from \[Merchant direct partnership / Affiliate network / Public aggregator\]."

\* Explicit merchant partnership list. Public page listing every merchant Groupon has an active affiliate / direct partnership with. Nothing else appears in the extension.

\* User dashboard for earned Bucks. Full transaction log \- when earned, what purchase, what merchant, what rate, when payable, when expired. Every Buck's provenance visible.

\* Change log for T\&C updates. Any change to rewards terms is published 30 days in advance with a summary of what changed.

What trust looks like in marketing

\* "Every merchant in the Groupon extension has a legitimate partnership with Groupon." (vs Honey's 146K/181K stores without consent)

\* "You can remove the Safari extension anytime, independently from the app." (vs Honey's bundle-lock)

\* "Your Bucks are yours. No silent expiration, no account-closure forfeiture, no fine print." (vs Honey's T\&Cs)

\* "You can cash out any balance above $1. It's your money." (vs Rakuten's $5.01 threshold)

\* "Codes we apply come from verified merchant partnerships. We never scrape codes you type." (vs Honey's MegaLag Part 2 allegations)

Why this matters economically

Trust is not a soft value. It has hard dollar consequences:

\* Honey lost 8M Chrome users in 12 months. At Honey's peak valuation implied $235 per user acquisition cost \[Data \- $4B PayPal acquisition / 17M MAU\], that's $1.88B of destroyed acquisition value. PayPal's 2025 public statements and the class action quantify the damage.

\* Three affiliate networks (Rakuten, Impact.com, Awin) terminated Honey in 5 days. Each of those relationships took years to build.

\* The class action is live, not dismissed. Honey's failure mode is now quantified in litigation.

For Groupon: the incremental cost of the trust-positive variant of each mechanic is tiny (\~10-15% extra eng work on each card). The benefit is avoiding the Honey trajectory.

Governance

\* Pre-launch review gate. Before any loyalty mechanic ships, a named reviewer (legal \+ PM \+ Adam Slaby) confirms it passes the checklist above.

\* Quarterly T\&C audit. A dedicated PM owns the loyalty T\&Cs and reviews them every quarter for language drift. Any change requires Adam Slaby \+ legal sign-off.

\* Annual mystery-shopper test. External contractor uses Groupon's cashback system end-to-end; publishes findings. Groupon acts on findings within 30 days. This is the public-trust proof.

\* Transparent metrics publication. Annually publish: % of earned Bucks actually paid out, % of merchants with direct / affiliate relationships vs listed, average tracking accuracy rate. Numbers are the anti-MegaLag vaccine.

Success metrics

\* NPS on trust categories ("I trust I will get paid", "The rewards are clear and fair") \- target: top quartile of consumer finance apps

\* Class actions filed against Groupon re: loyalty program \- target: zero

\* Affiliate network terminations / warnings \- target: zero

\* External mystery-shopper audit pass rate \- target: \>95%

Risks

\* Over-engineering trust at the expense of speed. If every mechanic goes through a 6-week review cycle, Groupon ships nothing. Mitigate with a default-approve checklist that 80% of decisions don't need senior review for.

\* Competitors under-invest in trust and ship faster. Possible. Counter-argument: Honey's 12-month exodus is why trust is the moat. Speed without trust is how you become Honey.

\* Regulatory ambiguity. "Industry standard" is a moving target. Stay ahead of it.

### **8\. Mechanic cards synthesis process**

*The six mechanic cards (\`mechanic-cards/01-quarterly-payout-ritual.md\` ... \`06-trust-and-transparency.md\`) are the most-reused synthesis output from this project. This section documents how they were produced, because the pattern is re-applicable to future competitor teardowns.*

### **Mechanic cards \- Skill used**

*\`startup:startup-competitors\` (in \`\~/.claude/plugins/.../startup/skills/startup-competitors/\`). The skill's default output is an investor-style battle card (price, positioning, sales angles). This project deviated from that default because Groupon is not selling against these competitors \- it is learning from them. The "battle card" was swapped for a "mechanic card" at synthesis time (Phase 3 pivot, see Iteration log).*

### **Mechanic cards \- Card template (9 sections)**

*Every card has the same shape so they can be scanned and compared side by side:*

*1\. What it is \- one-paragraph plain description of the mechanic*

*2\. Why it works (as a habit driver / loyalty mechanism) \- psychological or structural reason*

*3\. Evidence this works \- data points, survival signal, traffic metrics, anything supporting the claim*

*4\. Groupon adoption \- the recipe for implementing it, with explicit variant choices*

*5\. Specifically do NOT copy \- anti-patterns from the source competitor that would hurt Groupon*

*6\. Effort \- eng sprints, design, legal, marketing estimate*

*7\. Success metrics \- how to tell if it worked once shipped*

*8\. Risks \- what could go wrong, with mitigations*

*9\. Related \- pointers to other cards in the set that interact with this one*

*Discipline: if any of the 9 sections cannot be filled without guessing, it is better to mark the section \`\[DATA GAP\]\` than to invent content.*
